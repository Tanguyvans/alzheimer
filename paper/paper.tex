\begin{abstract}
Deep learning for Alzheimer's disease (AD) classification from MRI has shown promise, yet recent reviews reveal that fewer than 20\% of studies employ external validation, with data leakage (e.g., same subjects in train and test sets) inflating reported accuracies to 95-99\%. Studies with rigorous subject-level splitting achieve 66-90\%. We present an end-to-end multimodal deep learning framework that processes both 3D MRI scans and clinical tabular data. The framework combines a Vision Transformer (ViT) for imaging analysis with a Feature Tokenizer Transformer (FT-Transformer) for clinical features, fusing both modalities through bidirectional cross-attention. This multimodal fusion enables the model to leverage complementary information from structural brain imaging and patient clinical profiles. We validate on 6,065 subjects from three independent cohorts (ADNI, OASIS, NACC) using strict subject-level 5-fold cross-validation. Our approach achieves \textbf{92.4\% accuracy} (AUC: 0.96) for distinguishing cognitively normal subjects from AD-trajectory patients (including MCI patients who later developed AD), and \textbf{93.3\%} on established AD cases. External validation across datasets reveals that single-cohort models suffer severe performance degradation (accuracy drops of 25-43 points), while our multi-cohort approach maintains robust generalization. SHAP analysis recovers the canonical neuropsychological battery (executive function, semantic memory, working memory) on the tabular branch, and a causal region-wise ablation shows the imaging branch depends on temporal-lobe information: masking the temporal lobe drops mean AD probability from 77\% to 56\% on true-AD cases, while frontal-lobe masking has no effect.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia, contributing to 60-70\% of the 57 million dementia cases worldwide \cite{who2023}. Early and accurate diagnosis is crucial for patient care, clinical trials enrollment, and treatment planning. Early diagnosis increasingly relies on biomarkers---CSF analysis requiring lumbar puncture, or amyloid PET imaging with radioactive tracers---which are impractical for routine screening. Structural MRI combined with clinical tabular data offers a practical alternative, capturing characteristic patterns of brain atrophy alongside demographic and cognitive assessments without invasive procedures or radiation exposure.

Deep learning approaches for AD classification have proliferated, with many studies reporting accuracies exceeding 95\%. However, recent systematic reviews reveal that these numbers are often inflated by data leakage and insufficient external validation (details in Sec.~\ref{sec:methodological}).

Combining multiple data modalities has shown promise for improving AD classification. Clinical tabular data (demographics, cognitive assessments) provides complementary information to structural MRI. However, diagnostic screening scores like the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) are strongly correlated with cognitive status. MMSE, a 30-point clinician-administered test, showed median scores of 29 for cognitively normal (CN) subjects versus 23.5 for AD patients in the ADNI cohort \cite{qiu2020}. We explore combining modern transformer architectures---specifically ViT for 3D MRI and FT-Transformer for tabular data---while excluding such diagnostic screening scores (MMSE, CDR) to ensure the model learns from structural imaging patterns and neuropsychological assessments rather than diagnostic proxies.

Our contributions are: (1) an end-to-end multimodal framework combining a 3D ViT with MAE pretraining, an FT-Transformer for tabular data, and bidirectional cross-attention fusion; (2) validation on 6{,}065 subjects from three independent cohorts (ADNI, OASIS, NACC) with strict subject-level splitting; (3) cross-task generalization showing that training on the AD-trajectory (including MCI converters) improves established-AD detection to 93.3\% accuracy; and (4) an interpretability suite combining SHAP feature attribution, a causal region-wise ablation of the imaging branch, and a comparison against output-level fusion baselines, establishing that the cross-attention operates at the feature level and depends causally on temporal-lobe information.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Deep Learning for AD Classification}

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have dominated AD classification from MRI. Wen et al. \cite{wen2020} provided a comprehensive review, showing that 3D CNNs achieve up to 89\% balanced accuracy with proper methodology. More recently, Vision Transformers (ViT) \cite{dosovitskiy2021} have been adapted for 3D medical imaging. VECNN \cite{vecnn2024} combined ViT with CNNs, reaching 94.1\% on ADNI.

\subsection{Methodological Concerns}
\label{sec:methodological}

A critical issue in AD classification is data leakage, where test subjects' data inadvertently influences training. Yagis et al. \cite{yagis2021} quantified this effect: improper data splitting inflates accuracy by 25-55\%, with models achieving 96\% accuracy even on randomly assigned labels through memorization of individual brain morphology. Young et al. \cite{young2025} found that fewer than 20\% of published studies employ external validation, with studies lacking proper subject-level splitting reporting 95-99\% accuracy while rigorous studies achieve 66-90\%.

\subsection{Transformer Architectures}

Vision Transformers (ViT) \cite{dosovitskiy2021} divide images into patches, treating each as a token for self-attention. The self-attention mechanism allows direct modeling of long-range spatial dependencies, which may be beneficial for capturing distributed atrophy patterns characteristic of AD across multiple brain regions. Masked autoencoder (MAE) pretraining \cite{he2022} enables ViT to learn robust representations from unlabeled data by reconstructing masked patches. Kunanbayev et al. \cite{kunanbayev2024} explored MAE pretraining for medical imaging in low-data regimes, training ViT-Base models on medical datasets (BRATS 2023, IXI, OASIS3, HCP) with masking ratios of 25\%, 50\%, and 75\%. The 75\% masking ratio achieved the highest performance gains (up to 13.6\% improvement on ADNI), and the authors released pretrained weights which we use to initialize our MRI encoder.

For tabular data, the FT-Transformer \cite{gorishniy2021} embeds each feature as a separate token, enabling attention across features. This demonstrates strong performance compared to traditional MLPs across diverse tabular benchmarks.

\subsection{Multimodal Fusion}

Combining imaging with clinical data improves classification performance. Qiu et al. \cite{qiu2020} demonstrated this with an end-to-end FCN-MLP framework that achieved 96.8\% accuracy on ADNI by integrating MRI features with age, sex, and MMSE scores. However, diagnostic scores like MMSE (median: 29 for CN vs 23.5 for AD) are strongly predictive of cognitive status, raising concerns about whether such models learn disease patterns versus cognitive assessment correlations. In a larger follow-up, Qiu et al. \cite{qiu2022} scaled to 8,916 subjects across 8 cohorts using a hybrid architecture that concatenates CNN-derived imaging features with CatBoost for tabular data fusion. More recently, Golovanevsky et al.~\cite{maddi2022} introduced MADDi, which integrates imaging, clinical, and genetic features for AD diagnosis through cross-modal multi-head attention but uses 2D CNN-derived imaging features and is validated on a single cohort.

We advance this line of work by combining state-of-the-art transformer architectures for \emph{both} modalities and fusing them end-to-end: a 3D Vision Transformer (rather than CNN-derived features) with MAE pretraining for imaging, a Feature Tokenizer Transformer for clinical data, and bidirectional cross-attention fusion evaluated at multi-cohort scale.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Datasets}

We aggregated data from three publicly available cohorts:

\textbf{ADNI (Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative) \cite{adni2005}:} A longitudinal study collecting multimodal neuroimaging and clinical data to track Alzheimer's disease progression. We included 903 subjects (424 CN, 479 AD-trajectory).

\textbf{OASIS-3 (Open Access Series of Imaging Studies) \cite{oasis3}:} A longitudinal neuroimaging dataset from Washington University including cognitively normal older adults and individuals with cognitive decline. We included 1,030 subjects (742 CN, 288 AD-trajectory).

\textbf{NACC (National Alzheimer's Coordinating Center) \cite{nacc2007}:} The largest US Alzheimer's database, aggregating data from over 40 Alzheimer's Disease Research Centers (ADRCs). MRI data were obtained from SCAN (Standardized Centralized Alzheimer's \& Related Dementias Neuroimaging), NACC's centralized neuroimaging repository. We included 4,132 subjects (3,581 CN, 551 AD-trajectory).

\textbf{Classification Groups:} The AD-trajectory group includes both clinically diagnosed AD patients (established AD) and MCI subjects who subsequently progressed to AD (MCI converters). This grouping is grounded in the 2018 NIA-AA Research Framework \cite{jack2018}, which defines AD as a biological continuum rather than a clinical syndrome---individuals with AD pathology are on the Alzheimer's continuum regardless of clinical stage. Longitudinal neuroimaging studies have demonstrated that MCI converters exhibit AD-like atrophy patterns detectable on structural MRI at baseline \cite{jack2000,misra2009}. The combined dataset comprises 6,065 subjects (4,747 CN, 1,318 AD-trajectory: 78.3\% vs 21.7\%). Table~\ref{tab:dataset} summarizes the dataset characteristics.

\subsection{Data Splitting Strategy}

To prevent data leakage, we employ strict subject-level splitting: each subject appears in exactly one split, and only the first visit per subject is used to avoid temporal leakage from repeated scans. The 6{,}065 subjects are pooled and partitioned by label-stratified 5-fold cross-validation (80\% train+val / 20\% test per fold); within each fold the train set is split 90/10 into train/val, yielding 72\%/8\%/20\% splits (4{,}367/485/1{,}213 subjects). Experiments are repeated with 3 random seeds (42, 123, 456). Tabular feature standardization is fitted only on each fold's training split. This follows recommendations from Wen et al.\ \cite{wen2020} and addresses concerns raised by recent systematic reviews \cite{yagis2021}.

\subsection{MRI Preprocessing}

T1-weighted MRI scans underwent standardized preprocessing (Fig.~\ref{fig:preprocessing}): DICOM-to-NIfTI conversion, N4 bias field correction, non-linear SyN registration to MNI-152 (1.75\,mm isotropic), SynthStrip skull stripping \cite{synthstrip}, and resampling to $128^3$ voxels.

\subsection{Clinical Features}

We harmonized 16 clinical features consistently available across all three cohorts: 4 demographics (age, sex, education, marital status), 6 neuropsychological tests (category fluency, Trail Making A/B, digit span forward/backward, Boston Naming), 2 physical (weight, BMI), and 4 medical-history (alcohol, smoking, cardiovascular, neurological). Missing values were median-imputed and features z-score normalized. Cognitive screening scores (MMSE, CDR) were excluded to avoid learning test-score proxies of the diagnostic label.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}}
\caption{Multimodal fusion architecture combining Vision Transformer (ViT) for 3D MRI and FT-Transformer for clinical features with bidirectional cross-attention fusion.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

Our multimodal fusion architecture (Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}) consists of three components:

\textbf{MRI Encoder:} We use a Vision Transformer (ViT-Base) adapted for 3D volumes, initialized with MAE-pretrained weights from Kunanbayev et al. \cite{kunanbayev2024}. Input volumes are divided into 16$\times$16$\times$16 patches, each embedded into 768-dimensional tokens. The encoder consists of 12 transformer blocks with 12 attention heads and 768-dimensional embeddings.

\textbf{Tabular Encoder:} We employ the FT-Transformer \cite{gorishniy2021}, which embeds each feature independently before processing through transformer layers. We use 3 transformer blocks with 64-dimensional embeddings and 4 attention heads, producing a 64-dimensional output.

\textbf{Cross-Modal Fusion:} Both modality representations are fused using sequential bidirectional cross-attention with 8 heads and hidden dimension 512. MRI features first attend to tabular features, then tabular features attend to the enriched MRI representation:
\begin{align}
\hat{f}_{mri} &= \text{LN}(f_{mri} + \text{CrossAttn}(f_{mri}, f_{tab}, f_{tab})) \\
f'_{mri} &= \text{LN}(\hat{f}_{mri} + \text{FFN}(\hat{f}_{mri})) \\
\hat{f}_{tab} &= \text{LN}(f_{tab} + \text{CrossAttn}(f_{tab}, f'_{mri}, f'_{mri})) \\
f'_{tab} &= \text{LN}(\hat{f}_{tab} + \text{FFN}(\hat{f}_{tab})) \\
f_{fused} &= \text{MLP}([f'_{mri}; f'_{tab}])
\end{align}
where LN denotes layer normalization and semicolon denotes concatenation. Auxiliary classifiers on each branch (weighted 0.3) encourage modality-specific learning.

\subsection{Training Details}

Models were trained for up to 100 epochs with early stopping (patience 20 after min.\ 40 epochs), keeping the best-validation checkpoint. \textbf{Optimization:} AdamW ($\mathrm{wd}{=}0.01$), learning rate $2\!\times\!10^{-5}$ with 5-epoch linear warmup then cosine annealing to $10^{-7}$, layer-wise LR decay 0.75 for ViT, gradient clipping at norm 1.0. \textbf{Regularization:} weighted cross-entropy with inverse-frequency class weights and label smoothing ($\epsilon{=}0.1$), auxiliary modality-specific losses (weight 0.3), and test-time augmentation over 8 geometric transforms (original plus 7 flip combinations along axial/sagittal/coronal axes). Batch size 4 (3D-volume memory constraint), PyTorch 2.0 on a single NVIDIA GPU. All experiments used 5-fold CV repeated with 3 random seeds (42, 123, 456), yielding 15 training runs per configuration.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and AUC. Results are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation across 15 runs (5 folds $\times$ 3 seeds).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Classification Performance}

All models were trained on the \textbf{CN vs AD-trajectory} task. We evaluate on two test sets: (1) an in-domain AD-trajectory test set matching the training distribution, and (2) a cross-task \textbf{CN vs established AD} test set containing only established AD patients without MCI converters.

Table~\ref{tab:results} presents our main results. On the in-domain AD-trajectory task, our multimodal model achieved 92.4\% accuracy (sensitivity: 78.8\%, specificity: 96.2\%) with AUC of 0.959. The high specificity indicates strong performance in correctly identifying cognitively normal subjects, while the moderate sensitivity reflects the challenge of detecting early-stage disease presentations.

The same trajectory-trained model achieved \textbf{93.3\% accuracy} (sensitivity: 79.0\%, specificity: 96.2\%, AUC: 0.956) on the established AD test set. Notably, trajectory-based training achieves \textbf{superior sensitivity} (79.0\% vs 69.4\%) compared to training exclusively on established AD cases, while maintaining comparable specificity (96.2\% vs 97.4\%). Training on early-stage MCI converters teaches the model to detect subtle atrophy patterns, improving sensitivity on established AD cases.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

To quantify the contribution of each modality, we evaluated single-modality baselines using identical training protocols on the AD-trajectory task (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). All models used 5-fold cross-validation with 3 random seeds. The MRI-only model (ViT with MAE pretraining) achieved 83.3\% accuracy (sensitivity: 56.2\%, specificity: 90.8\%) with AUC of 0.826. The tabular-only model (FT-Transformer) achieved 84.9\% accuracy (sensitivity: 83.5\%, specificity: 85.2\%) with AUC of 0.925.

The multimodal fusion achieves substantial gains over both unimodal baselines: \textbf{+9.1\%} accuracy over MRI-only and \textbf{+7.5\%} over tabular-only. Critically, the multimodal approach achieves \textbf{96.2\% specificity}---substantially higher than either modality alone---demonstrating that cross-attention fusion learns to leverage complementary strengths. The MRI-only model exhibits low sensitivity (56.2\%), suggesting that structural imaging alone struggles with early-stage detection. Conversely, tabular features achieve balanced sensitivity/specificity (83.5\%/85.2\%), confirming that cognitive assessments and clinical variables carry significant diagnostic value.

The results reveal substantial cross-cohort generalization gaps for single-cohort models: while ADNI-only training achieves 81.4$\pm$3.5\% accuracy in-domain, performance drops to 56.5$\pm$9.2\% on OASIS (-24.9 points) and 38.5$\pm$13.9\% on NACC (-42.9 points). The high variance on external cohorts (SD up to 13.9\%) reflects inconsistent generalization across folds. NACC exhibits notable miscalibration despite high AUC (0.89): the model achieves 98\% sensitivity but only 29\% specificity, incorrectly classifying 70\% of healthy controls as diseased. This arises from class distribution shift (ADNI: 47\% CN; NACC: 87\% CN), causing decision threshold mismatch. These results demonstrate that single-cohort models overfit to scanner-specific and population-specific patterns. By comparison, our multi-cohort approach achieves \textbf{92.4\% accuracy}---surpassing even ADNI in-domain performance by 11 points---by learning cohort-invariant features from diverse acquisition protocols and demographics, confirming that multi-cohort training is essential for clinically deployable AD classification systems.

\subsection{Interpretability}
\label{subsec:interpretability}

We conducted three complementary analyses on the cross-validation checkpoints (seed 42) to understand how the model makes its decisions and combines modalities: (i) SHAP feature importance on the tabular branch, (ii) ViT spatial attention on the imaging branch, and (iii) cross-modal concordance against independently-trained unimodal baselines on a representative fold.

\subsubsection{Tabular Feature Importance via SHAP}

We apply SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017shap} (GradientExplainer, 100-sample training reference) to the tabular sub-network and report the mean $\lvert\text{SHAP}\rvert$ on the AD-class logit averaged across 5 folds (Fig.~\ref{fig:shap_tabular}); cross-fold sign consistency was too low (median 3/5) to support directional claims, so we restrict interpretation to importance magnitude. Four of the top five features---\textbf{TMT-B} (executive function, rank 1), \textbf{Boston Naming} (language, rank 2), \textbf{Category Fluency} (semantic memory, rank 3), and \textbf{Digit Span Backward} (working memory, rank 5)---constitute the canonical neuropsychological battery used in AD diagnosis. Neurological history (rank 4) and age (rank 6) contribute at an intermediate level, while non-specific risk factors (smoking, cardiac history, alcohol) occupy the last three positions, supporting that the model relies on clinically-meaningful signals rather than demographic proxies.

\subsubsection{Spatial Brain Attention}

Attention rollout~\cite{abnar2020quantifying} on the most confidently-classified subjects per fold shows AD attention concentrating around temporal-lobe regions (peak 3.6$\times$ higher than CN), but attention weights alone are not reliable evidence of causal use. We therefore ran a \emph{region-wise ablation} on fold~0's test subjects ($N=1{,}213$): for each subject we replaced voxels inside the Harvard-Oxford temporal lobe with the subject's brain-mean intensity and measured the change in the model's AD probability, using the frontal lobe as a neutral control (Fig.~\ref{fig:vit_attn}). On true-AD subjects ($N=263$), the model's mean AD probability \textbf{drops from 77\% to 56\%} when the temporal lobe is masked, while it remains at 78\% when the frontal lobe is masked ($247/263$ subjects show a larger temporal drop, paired-t $p<10^{-16}$). The model's AD decisions therefore depend \emph{causally} on temporal-lobe information rather than merely attending to it---consistent with the earliest sites of AD-related atrophy.

\subsubsection{Cross-modal Contribution}

To isolate the contribution of the cross-attention fusion \emph{beyond} what any output-level combination of the two unimodal predictions could achieve, we compared our fusion (93.4\%) against four alternatives on fold 0's held-out subjects ($N=1{,}213$): each unimodal alone (MRI-only ViT 82.7\%, tabular-only FT-Transformer 84.1\%), probability averaging of the two, and a logistic-regression stacker trained on the two unimodal outputs via cross-validation. The per-regime breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:concordance}) shows where the gain comes from. When unimodals disagree ($N=307$), probability averaging collapses to near-chance (48.5\%) and the stacker reaches 78.2\%, while our fusion arbitrates correctly in \textbf{87.6\%} of cases. More striking, among the 48 subjects where \emph{both} unimodals are confidently wrong, the fusion still recovers \textbf{43.8\%} of the ground-truth label while probability averaging recovers 0\% and stacking 4.2\%. Methods that only see the unimodal outputs cannot rescue cases where both are wrong; cross-attention, operating on internal representations, can.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Cross-Task Generalization}

Our results reveal that training on the heterogeneous AD-trajectory task (including MCI converters) yields superior performance for established AD detection compared to training exclusively on established AD cases. Models trained on AD-trajectory achieve 0.956 AUC on established AD versus 0.948 for models trained directly on established AD (Table~\ref{tab:results}), with significantly higher sensitivity (79.0\% vs 69.4\%) despite slightly lower specificity (96.2\% vs 97.4\%).

This demonstrates that training on challenging early-stage cases forces the model to learn discriminative features that generalize to pronounced disease. MCI converters exhibit subtle atrophy patterns near the noise floor of normal aging variability, requiring fine-grained feature extraction. When applied to established AD cases with pronounced atrophy, these learned features transfer effectively without relying on gross anatomical changes. The trajectory-trained model achieves 93.3\% accuracy on established AD detection, only 0.9 points below its in-domain performance (92.4\%), supporting the clinical utility of heterogeneous training for robust early detection.

\subsection{Clinical Integration}

The interpretability analyses suggest a concrete deployment pathway. Tabular features can be extracted automatically from electronic health records, while MRI interpretation involves standard acquisition and radiologist reading. Our model would serve as decision support by (i) flagging high-risk tabular profiles for prioritized imaging review, (ii) providing per-subject spatial attention maps (Fig.~\ref{fig:vit_attn}) that the radiologist can corroborate or override, and (iii) exposing per-feature SHAP importance (Fig.~\ref{fig:shap_tabular}) so clinicians verify the reasoning. When the two modalities disagree (25\% of cases), the fusion's arbitration acts as an explicit second opinion rather than a black-box prediction.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

\textbf{Class imbalance:} The 78\%/22\% CN/AD ratio affects sensitivity. Future work could explore oversampling or focal loss.

\textbf{Missing data:} Some tabular features have incomplete coverage across cohorts (23-100\%), handled via median imputation which may lose information.

\textbf{Interpretability:} Our analyses (Sec.~\ref{subsec:interpretability}) are population-level; low cross-fold sign consistency of SHAP values limits directional claims for individual features. Per-subject SHAP dependence plots or Integrated Gradients could provide patient-specific attributions for clinical decision support.

\textbf{External validation:} While our cross-cohort experiment (Table~\ref{tab:external}) demonstrates the importance of multi-cohort training, all three cohorts participate in cross-validation. A fully independent held-out cohort used only for testing would provide stronger generalization evidence.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a multimodal deep learning framework for Alzheimer's disease classification combining Vision Transformer and Feature Tokenizer Transformer with cross-attention fusion. Validated on 6,065 subjects from three independent cohorts with strict subject-level splitting, our approach achieves 92.4\% accuracy on the challenging AD-trajectory task and 93.3\% on established AD detection. Multimodal fusion provides substantial gains over unimodal baselines (+9.1\% over MRI-only, +7.5\% over tabular-only). Training on heterogeneous AD-trajectory cases yields improved generalization to established AD (AUC: 0.956 vs 0.948), demonstrating that early-stage training enhances late-stage detection. External validation experiments confirm the necessity of multi-cohort training for robust clinical deployment, with single-cohort models exhibiting performance drops of 25-43 points on held-out cohorts.
